Pipeline cost & resource model

Open-weight vision-language models run on a single owned DGX Spark at the BBAW server room. LLM APIs are used selectively for disputed sentences and bibliographic extraction. Human effort is dominated by a fixed team doing daily QC-correction work, with effort decreasing once enough training data accumulates to deploy a fine-tuned local model.

Akademie Vorhaben standard

OCR pipeline architecture: open-weight vision-language models (Chandra-2, Qwen2.5-VL, InternVL3) deployed locally on owned hardware. Phase 1 infrastructure is a single NVIDIA DGX Spark (Grace-Blackwell, 128 GB unified memory; ~€5,000 hardware investment, hosted at BBAW server room as institutional infrastructure). Models are run in self-consensus mode (same model, multiple stochastic passes) and cross-model consensus, voting on disputed sentences only. LLM API access is used selectively for localized consensus resolution and header-coordinate detection (~€2,000 across the project lifetime). The 3-way diff verification interface simultaneously serves three purposes: quality assurance, generation of (image, text) training pairs in EIS1600 mARkdown format, and iterative fine-tuning of open-weight models calibrated for classical Arabic typography. Header and poetry structural tags are explicitly captured as primary annotation targets, supporting downstream computational analysis. Fine-tuning runs are executed on owned hardware or via no-cost academic compute clusters. By Phase 2, fine-tuned local models are projected to handle the majority of pages without consensus, reducing per-page processing time by 5–10× and producing a redistributable Arabic-OCR model as a public research output. ACO holdings come from Arabic Collections Online, hosted by NYU Libraries (NYU Abu Dhabi), with contributing partners at AUB, AUC, Columbia, Cornell, Princeton, and the UAE National Archives. Shamela PDFs records are from the Shamela digital library. HathiTrust integration is planned for Phase 3 via direct partnership negotiation.

Corpus composition — discipline distribution

Three views of the same corpus, revealing why computational typology is necessary.

Shamela PDFs — emic disciplines (41 categories)

Shamela's tradition-internal classification — granular but inconsistent (e.g., author corpora as a category).

ACO — Library of Congress top-level (~20 real categories)

Western library classification — collapses tafsīr, ḥadīṯ, fiqh, kalām, and devotional literature into a single "Religion" bucket (~28% of all ACO records).

Waqfeya — Dewey-like Arabic adaptation (~130 categories)

Al-Maktaba al-Waqfiyya's catalog scheme — Dewey decimal numbers prefixed onto Arabic-language category labels. More granular than LC for Islamic disciplines (madhhab-level fiqh, distinct ḥadīṯ subgenres), still less emic than Shamela's tradition-internal vocabulary.

Unified 14-bucket taxonomy — ACO vs Shamela vs Waqfeya

Coarse mapping of all three schemes onto a shared Arabic-tradition vocabulary. "(مختلط) / mixed" buckets are explicitly marked: they reveal where Library of Congress over-generalizes and where the more emic schemes have no equivalent.

ACO Shamela PDFs Waqfeya

These four views together demonstrate why none of the existing classification schemes is adequate for the computational study of the Arabic written tradition. Shamela PDFs over-specifies along confessional and authorial lines: madhhab-specific fiqh, dedicated author corpora (Ibn Taymiyya, al-Albānī, Ibn al-Qayyim, Ibn Abī al-Dunyā), and small subgenres that reflect the catalogers' theological priorities rather than the tradition's intellectual geography. The Library of Congress, applied to ACO, over-generalizes in the opposite direction: nearly 5,000 volumes (28% of the collection) collapse into a single "Philosophy, Psychology, Religion" bucket, fusing exegesis, ḥadīṯ studies, theology, law, devotional literature, and Sufism into one undifferentiated category. Al-Maktaba al-Waqfiyya's Dewey-derived scheme sits between the two: more granular than LC for Islamic disciplines (separate buckets for the four Sunnī madhhabs, distinct ḥadīṯ subgenres), still less emic than Shamela's tradition-internal vocabulary, and importing Dewey's Western-encyclopedic top-level structure into Arabic-language cataloging. The mapping table above is a least-common-denominator alignment; the asymmetry between the three sources — particularly the size of the "(مختلط) / mixed" buckets and the silence of Western schemes about most of the intra-Islamic distinctions Shamela makes explicit — is itself the strongest argument that a computational typology, calibrated to the formal and rhetorical properties of the works themselves, is required for research on the tradition.
